HOMO TURENSIS

The Mechanics of the Civilization of Spivbuttya (Co-being)

The Attention Limit
For the first time in history, information arrives faster than humanity can turn it into culture. Once, knowledge accumulated slowly, and each generation had time to make sense of what the last had left — to weave it into language, craft, law, custom. Now the stream outruns understanding. We produce more than we can read; we store more than we can remember; we connect more than we can know. For the first time, civilization creates information faster than it turns it into meaning.
The temptation is to blame the devices. Social networks. Recommendation feeds. Artificial intelligence. But that is a mistake of scale: they are not the cause, only the accelerators. The cause runs deeper, and it lies not in the screen but in the human being.
The human being has a limit. He does not read faster than he reads, does not think endlessly longer, does not hold thousands of deep bonds at once, does not pour an infinite stream into himself without losing the ability to tell the essential from the noise. This is not weakness of character or want of discipline — it is a physical limit, the same as the limit of sight or hearing. The bandwidth of attention is finite.
And here two curves collide. The first is the number of interactions, and it flies upward by a relentless law: any cheapening of interaction multiplies interactions. The letter. The printing press. The telegraph. The telephone. The internet. The smartphone. Every invention cheapened connection — and every one multiplied connections. The second curve is the capacity to make sense of those interactions, and it is almost flat, because it runs up against biology. The first line climbs steeply, the second barely rises, and the space between them grows every year. That space is the crisis. Until recently this first curve still rested on the human being: information was produced by a human, and so its production was bounded by the same cognition as its absorption. Both curves grew from a single root. Now, for the first time, the source has broken free. Information begins to be produced by the machine, and its production no longer runs up against the human mind — it runs up only against computing power, and that has almost no limit. One curve snaps its tether and flies upward on its own; the other stays chained to biology. The gap between them ceases to be linear. From here it begins to grow faster than ever before in history.
Homo Sapiens has reached his limit. Not through a lack of resources — there are more of them than ever. Not through a lack of knowledge — there is more of it than humanity can read. He has reached the limit of his own capacity to integrate what he himself produces. For the first time, civilization is bounded not by what it lacks, but by what it has too much of.
And then what we are used to calling cultural decline takes on another aspect. "Brain rot" is not an illness or a moral defeat. It is the natural state of a system whose flow of information has exceeded its capacity to turn that flow into meaning. Not a cause, but a symptom. Once you see it that way, it sounds almost like a formula: the mind grows dim not because it degrades, but because it is drowning.
So a long age comes to an end. For millennia humanity lived in a scarcity of information, and all its institutions learned to extract it, store it, transmit it, guard it. Now the scarcity has flipped: information is in surplus, attention is what runs short. And the moment the scarcest resource changes, another war ignites over it. States, platforms, markets, ideologies no longer fight first of all over oil, territory, or even money. They fight over attention — because now it is the rarest resource, and therefore the greatest power.
Hence what is worth saying plainly, for in it lies the whole diagnosis of the present. Whoever defines the configuration of attention defines civilization. Not the one who owns the resource. Not the one who controls the territory. The one who sets what the field looks at. The entire struggle of Homo Sapiens at his limit is a struggle for the right to place that focus. And as long as the focus can be seized and held in a single point, civilization remains governed from outside — and, for that very reason, vulnerable.
For civilization, too, is a system for processing information. It takes in, filters, stores, and integrates — only not in one head, but in millions, connected to one another. And, like a single human being, it has a finite bandwidth — its own collective attention, its own limit to what it can hold in focus at once. But this limit is built differently.
The bandwidth of a human being is set by biology. The bandwidth of a civilization is set by the quality of the connection between people.
This is where the true limit runs. The biology of a single head cannot be raised — a human will not read faster. But the quality of the connection between heads can be raised without limit, and a civilization can coordinate more exactly to the degree that the losses between its nodes are smaller. So the way out of the crisis is not to speed up the human being. It is to raise the conductivity between human beings.
And here the last thing opens up. Conductivity cannot be bought, nor commanded, nor built in by technology. It arises only between those who answer the same way the question of what is to be counted as real at all. Two people for whom the world is made of different things will conduct almost nothing to each other — however many channels you lay between them. The fastest network is powerless where there is no shared answer to the simplest question: what is. And so the deepest limit of a civilization lies not in its technology, not in its politics, not in its economy — it lies in how a civilization divides the world into what exists and what does not.
Now the sequence in which everything falls into place becomes visible. Energy sets the physical scale of a civilization — how much it is able to make. Information sets its intellectual scale — how much it is able to know. Attention sets its coordination scale — how much it is able to reconcile at once. And beneath all three lies one more limit, deeper than energy, information, and attention together — and it is on this limit that they all stand. It belongs neither to technology, nor to politics, nor to economics. And if the crisis is not there and not there, one place remains where it can be hiding.
And only now, when the problem is seen rather than announced, does the word appear by which it can be named.
Ontology.

Law I. Ontology of a civilization
The ontology of a civilization is its answer to a single question: what is the fundamental unit of reality? Every institution, every form of coordination, every economic and political structure grows from that answer. Politics, economics, law, technology, culture, and education do not create a civilization—they are expressions of its ontology.
And each answer cuts its own riverbed. Ontology gives rise to a mechanics of coordination, the mechanics to institutions, institutions to history. History founds nothing; it only gathers the consequences, as a river gathers silt where the water has already passed.
For several millennia Homo Sapiens ran his current down the riverbed of separateness. The human being became the base unit; the state coordinated people, law coordinated states, the market coordinated property, the corporation coordinated capital, education coordinated knowledge. Each successive institution widened the one before it without changing the original geometry of the flow. The scale grew. The riverbed stayed the same.
Behind this stands a single relentless cycle. Any cheapening of interaction multiplies interactions; any multiplication of interactions builds complexity; any complexity raises the bar of coordination — and so round and round, without cease. The whole history of Homo Sapiens fits within this turning. The steam engine widened production, electricity — industry, computation came with the computer, information poured in with the internet, and now artificial intelligence pushes apart the very riverbed of decision-making. And yet none of these waves has touched ontology — each only drove the water faster down a channel already cut.
Separateness gave civilization an unheard-of productivity. It also drew the ceiling of its coordination capacity. Each new technological layer speeds the current, but the banks stay the same, and past a certain scale something strange happens: institutions begin to coordinate no longer interaction, but one another. The state coordinates the state, law coordinates law, finance coordinates finance, algorithms coordinate algorithms. An ever-greater share of the energy goes not to moving forward, but to holding up the riverbed the civilization cut for itself.
The passage between civilizations begins not with a change of institutions — it begins with a change of ontology. And the moment it shifts, every old institution becomes a special case of the new mechanics. The state ceases to be the source of order and becomes one of the possible mechanisms of coordination. The market ceases to be the universal model of exchange and becomes one of the ways of distributing value. Democracy becomes one of the ways of synchronizing decisions, and technology no longer sets the direction — it sets only the speed at which the new ontology spreads through the world.
Turism describes the ontology of Spivbuttya. Co-being is not added to the world as one more principle — it draws the very riverbed within which any form becomes possible at all. Form localizes interaction, interaction — conductivity, conductivity — consciousness, consciousness — civilization. Ontology no longer begins with the object. It begins with the field.
From this law arises Homo Turensis.









Law II. Coordination
Coordination defines the limit of a civilization. Not territory, not population, not the store of resources — the limit runs where the mechanism of reconciliation ceases to keep pace with the interactions it itself gave rise to. Past that point a civilization goes on building scale for a long time, but ever more of its strength ebbs from development to the upkeep of what is already built. Different ages tell this story in different words, and the story is one.
Coordination scales in layers. The family conducts immediate experience, the tribe — trust, the city — specialization, the state — coercion, the market — exchange, the network — information. No layer cancels the one before it; it only raises its scale — and with scale grows the distance between cause and effect. The human being ceases to see the full result of his own act, because the decision now flows through an ever-longer chain of intermediaries. Inertia grows, delay grows, the cost of reconciliation grows. Direct interaction gives way to the management of abstractions.
And each institution, cutting one cost, gives rise to another. Money cheapens exchange — and financial coordination grows dearer. Law cheapens agreement — and legal complexity grows dearer. The state cheapens the centralized decision — and its adaptation grows dearer. The internet cheapens the transmission of information — and the verification of its truth grows dearer. Artificial intelligence cheapens cognitive labor — and the reconciliation between human and machine grows dearer. No cutting is ever free; the civilization only endlessly shifts the costs from level to level.
Complexity grows not from technology. It grows from successful coordination — because each good decision opens room for a still greater number of interactions. That is why productivity and complexity rise together, on one wave. And a moment comes when the chief resource is no longer energy, no longer material, no longer information, but the capacity itself to conduct an ever-greater stream of interactions without loss of conductivity. Here coordination ceases to be a technical function and becomes the fundamental law of development.
The ontology of Homo Sapiens coordinates separate forms. Turism coordinates the interaction between forms. The difference is not one of worldview — it is one of unit. If the unit is the separate human being, civilization builds up mechanisms for governing people. If the unit is Spivbuttya, civilization builds up mechanisms for holding conductivity between them. And then the direction of every institution turns at once. Trust ceases to be psychology and becomes a coordination resource. Education ceases to transmit knowledge and begins to raise the coherence of thinking. Economics ceases to count what is stored and begins to measure the quality of circulation. Politics ceases to divide power and begins to hold conductivity between the levels of civilization.
And then coordination ceases to be a function of the center — it becomes a property of the field. The field makes no decisions; it only shows where a decision will be born with the least loss of current. The source of coordination is no longer fixed in place: it shifts along with the environment, the task, the competence, the scale. Stability breaks free of the immutability of the center and begins to live in something else — in the ceaseless movement of the center following the structure of interaction, like the thread of a current that finds for itself the deepest place in the channel. From this law arises the next mechanics — the mechanics of power.
















Law III. Power
Power is born as a consequence of coordination. No civilization creates power for the sake of power — it builds a mechanism able to shorten the time between an event and a reconciled decision. While interactions are few, reconciliation arises directly between the participants. The moment the scale grows, decisions begin to gather in separate nodes, and so the center arises. The center is not a political invention but a coordination optimization. Any civilization in the riverbed of separateness inevitably draws ever more decisions into an ever-smaller number of points.
Centralization shortens the time of a decision exactly as long as the environment changes more slowly than the center works. Past that limit the center begins to accumulate delay. Each new decision demands more information, more approvals, more levels of management — and the node that arose as a remedy against complexity slowly becomes its chief source. Power takes to coordinating its own existence. In that moment the main strength of civilization ebbs to the upkeep of centers rather than to conductivity between participants — the water silts up its own channel.
The political model here means almost nothing. Monarchy draws decisions into the figure of the ruler, parliament — into procedure, the corporation — into management, the platform — into the algorithm. The form changes, the mechanics remain: the center always does one thing — it localizes coordination. That is why the history of power is not a struggle of ideologies but an evolution of the ways of drawing coordination capacity into a single point.
Turism flows otherwise. Coordination does not settle in a permanent center — it flares up at the point of highest conductivity. And conductivity changes along with the environment, and with it the source of coordination wanders. The center ceases to be an institution and becomes a temporary function of the field: it exists while it holds the least loss, and the moment the structure of interaction shifts — it passes to another node. Power ceases to accumulate, because it no longer has a permanent place to settle.
From this mechanics arises pupsocracy. Coordination binds itself no longer to a person, a post, or an organization, but to the capacity itself to hold conductivity — here, at this scale, in this moment. The decision passes to that node through which the field reconciles itself with the least loss, and the moment the task is solved — the center vanishes together with the function it carried. Civilization ceases to search for an unchanging helmsman. It sustains the ceaseless movement of coordination following the change of the field — so the thread of the current, moment by moment, finds anew where the channel runs deepest.
Pupsocracy does not divide power. It changes the unit in which power exists at all. The unit ceases to be the right to govern; the unit becomes the capacity to reconcile interaction. And in the ontology of Spivbuttya this capacity cannot be permanent, because the field itself changes ceaselessly. Power takes on the properties of a flow: it localizes, performs a function, flows on. Keeping conductivity matters more than keeping the center — and this is what defines the political mechanics of Homo Turensis.
The next law describes the mechanics of trust. A civilization scales exactly as far as trust scales; all other mechanisms are only ways of substituting for it or holding it.
















Law IV. Conductivity
Conductivity defines the furthest limit of coordination. Any interaction begins with the assumption that the other side is predictable. If that assumption holds for free, coordination flows straight through. If not, a mechanism of compensation grows up between the participants. Law compensates for the want of trust through coercion, the contract — through formalization, the court — through arbitration, the bank — through guarantee, reputation — through the history of conduct, cryptography — through mathematical proof. Civilization endlessly forges instruments to cheapen mistrust.
And each such instrument has its price. Law builds up legal complexity, the bank — a file of intermediaries, administration — bureaucracy, the platform — informational asymmetry. The greater the scale, the more mechanisms hold the interaction between people who can no longer know one another directly. The dearer trust becomes, the more strength a civilization diverts from the making of value to the upkeep of the props that stand in for its want.
Separateness lodges trust inside the human being: this one trusts, that one does not, and all institutions are raised around this assumption. Turism changes the very unit of description. Trust is a property of interaction, not a store inside the head; it is not kept in a person and not passed from hand to hand, but defines the quality of conductivity of the field of Spivbuttya itself. The separate human being only localizes this quality in a particular touch. And the moment the unit of description changes, the whole architecture changes: the central resource becomes no longer control over the participants, but conductivity between them.
Conductivity is the capacity of the field to conduct intention, information, responsibility, and value without accumulating inner resistance. In physics the loss goes to friction; in a civilization — to the growth of the cost of coordination. Any institution raises conductivity as long as it cuts the total loss of interaction. And having outgrown its own scale, it begins to work in reverse: coordination clogs, intermediaries multiply, the noise thickens, and local optimizations, one after another, cease to coincide with the optimization of the whole. The civilization still works, but ever more of its energy scatters within its own riverbed, like a current spending itself on friction against its own banks.
Homo Turensis grows from the other end. Growth begins not with the institution but with conductivity. First the interaction is tied; from the repeated interaction trust is born; trust thickens into a stable node of coordination; nodes weave into an enclave, enclaves into a confederation, the confederation into a civilization. The direction of building is reversed: the institution no longer precedes interaction but arises later — as the stabilization of what has already reached sufficient conductivity.
Hence another law of scale. The growth of a civilization is measured no longer by the size of a population, but by the number of interactions able to hold high conductivity despite geography, language, culture, or state belonging. Homo Turensis grows neither through centralization nor through fragmentation, but through the synchronization of autonomous nodes that share a common ontology. A shared ontology cheapens coordination more powerfully than any legal or administrative mechanism — simply because it removes the need to agree anew about the nature of reality before every new touch.
The next law describes the mechanics of the enclave. A civilization scales not as a continuous territory but as a network of local fields of Spivbuttya, each holding the same ontology under full autonomy of its own development.













Law V. Enclave
A civilization scales through repeated interaction, and no institution stands in for its absence. People may obey one law, carry one currency, live on one land — and belong to different civilizations. And the reverse: they may never meet in the body, speak different languages, grow up in different cultures — and be one civilization, if their interaction is conducted by a common ontology. Geography measures the distance between bodies. Ontology measures the distance between mechanics of coordination.
The enclave is the smallest stable unit of Homo Turensis. It is defined not by territory, not by legal status, not by form of ownership, but by one thing alone: the capacity to hold conductivity among all its participants. Any growth that lowers this conductivity buds off a new enclave; any narrowing that impoverishes the diversity of interactions — likewise. The size of an enclave is not set by decree. It arises on its own, as a property of the current — and so different enclaves may be of different scale and remain one ontology.
The enclave does not crave self-sufficiency. Full self-sufficiency buys local resilience at the price of specialization; full specialization buys productivity at the price of autonomy. Homo Turensis holds another balance: enough autonomy to exist without cease, and enough openness to exchange without cease with others. The flow of people, knowledge, resources, technologies, cultural practices does not tear the wholeness of the enclave, because the ontology stays common. Coordination precedes exchange — exchange only embodies what has already been reconciled.
The enclave is not a hermit community but a local node of the planetary field of Spivbuttya. Each node grows its own culture, its own rituals, its own economy, its own technical solutions, its own way of laying out space. And this motleyness does not splinter the civilization — it makes it hardy. Unification narrows the set of possible answers to a change of environment; the diversity of enclaves multiplies the chance that at least one of them will feel out a more effective mechanics, which will then flow through the whole confederation.
So the enclave ceases to be an administrative unit and becomes a unit of experiment. Each one endlessly tests new ways to coordinate, produce, teach, heal, build, feed with energy. And no solution scales by command — it scales by conductivity: if the new mechanics raises coordination capacity, the other enclaves take it up on their own; if not, it stays a local experience. The evolution of a civilization passes from centralized planning to a ceaseless distributed search.
So the enclave ceases to be a place of residence as well. It becomes a mechanism for the localization of Spivbuttya. A person is born in one enclave, studies in another, works in a third, researches in a fourth, coordinates joint projects with dozens of the rest. The civilization ceases to identify a person with the land and begins to identify her with the configuration of interactions she holds. Identity slowly resettles from the place of birth to the structure of conductivity.
The enclave is not the final form of Homo Turensis but its elementary cell. No cell alone is an organism; the organism lives in the coordination between cells. In the same way, the civilization of Homo Turensis lives not in a separate enclave but in the mechanics of interaction between them. This mechanics defines the next law — the law of the confederation.

















Law VI. Confederation
A confederation arises when it is cheaper to coordinate enclaves than to centralize them. This is not a political decision but a property of the current. Centralization cuts costs as long as one center manages to pass most interactions through itself. Past that scale, each new level of centralization only adds delay: the system still works, but loses flexibility. The confederation localizes coordination where the interaction is born. The center ceases to accumulate decisions and begins to accumulate only the protocols of interaction between autonomous nodes.
Autonomy here does not mean isolation. A closed system exhausts its own diversity, a thoroughly centralized one — its own flexibility, while the confederation holds ceaseless exchange between enclaves without taking from them the right to a local decision. Through this the civilization at once multiplies diversity and holds wholeness — and there is no contradiction in this, because these properties live on different levels of one mechanics: local autonomy multiplies possible answers, common ontology cheapens their synchronization.
The unity of a confederation is held not by land but by the immutability of ontology. An enclave may stand in a desert, in a megacity, in the mountains, on a floating settlement, on an orbital station; its architecture, economy, climate, technological level may differ utterly. As long as the mechanics of Turism is common, interaction between enclaves needs no re-agreement of the fundamentals. Coordination begins not with negotiations about the nature of reality, but straight from the solving of a concrete task.
A confederation does not produce sameness — it produces compatibility. Sameness impoverishes the set of answers; compatibility lets different answers live in one field. A living ecosystem is made not of identical organisms but of ones that hold a common cycle of life — and Homo Turensis flows down that same riverbed. Enclaves may raise different educational systems, different economic models, different technical protocols, different cultures; the confederation only holds their mutual compatibility, without intruding into local evolution.
The scale of a confederation is measured not by the number of participants but by the speed with which an effective mechanics flows through it. The moment one enclave feels out a way to cut the losses of coordination, the others take it up with no command from above. It is not power that flows — it is mechanics. The confederation becomes a ceaseless system of civilizational learning, where each local experiment is a potential update of the whole. Evolution passes from political competition to a competition of mechanics of coordination.
The territorial state holds by the border; the confederation holds by the membrane. The border divides spaces — the membrane conducts flows. Through it flow people, knowledge, energy, resources, technologies, cultural practices, and passage is defined not by belonging but by the compatibility of mechanics. The civilization ceases to build itself around the control of space and begins to build itself around the reconciliation of flows.
So the confederation accumulates not sovereignty but conductivity. Each new enclave adds not area but coordination capacity. Growth is measured no longer by territory, population, or gross product, but by the number of autonomous nodes able to hold a common ontology without losing their own uniqueness. The civilization passes from a geographic map to a topological one: on it, state borders are no longer visible — the structure of conductivity between enclaves is.
The confederation sets the architecture of Homo Turensis; the next law sets its operating mechanism. Coordination between autonomous enclaves needs an environment where the rules of interaction live independently of any separate institution. This role is carried by protocols. From their mechanics arises the DAO.










Law VII. Protocol
Any civilization flows through protocols. Law is a protocol, money is a protocol, language is a protocol, the scientific method is a protocol, the market is a protocol. A protocol sets not the result but the sequence of interaction in which independent participants arrive at a compatible result without ceaseless oversight from outside. The more precisely it works, the less a center of coordination is needed.
A protocol needs no trust in a particular person — it needs trust in its own reproducibility. That is why the whole history of civilization is a history of protocols. Writing let knowledge be coordinated beyond the memory of a single person, law — conduct beyond the will of a single ruler, money — the economy beyond personal relations, the internet — information beyond physical distance. Each successful protocol tears interaction away from personality, and each new one raises the scale of civilization a rung higher.
And yet a protocol does not cancel trust — it relocates it. Trust descends from the particular bearer of power and flows onto the mechanics of interaction; and if the mechanics reproduces itself independently of the participants, the civilization slowly pours ever more coordination from institutions into protocols. This process ran through the whole history of Homo Sapiens — the digital age only made it visible.
The DAO is a protocol of coordination. Its essence is not in the blockchain, not in the cryptocurrency, not in the voting; all of these are only particular embodiments. The essence is in something else: the rules of interaction cease to depend on a permanent center of management and live as an open protocol, equally available to every node of the network. Coordination resettles from the organization into the mechanics. That is why the DAO is not a new form of company but a new way for the institution as such to exist.
Homo Turensis takes the DAO not as a political model but as a direct consequence of its own ontology. If Spivbuttya is primary, no permanent center stays optimal for all interactions at once; coordination passes to that node which holds the highest conductivity in a concrete process, and the protocol only lets this passage happen without loss of compatibility with the rest. Power now moves not through struggle but through a change in the configuration of the field.
And then the DAO ceases to be a separate organization. Every enclave is a DAO, every research group is a DAO, every production cycle, every educational project — a DAO, and even the confederation itself flows as a DAO of a higher scale. The organization is defined no longer by legal form but by a protocol of coordination. One and the same participant takes part at once in dozens of DAOs of different scale without changing his civilizational belonging. The civilization passes from a tree of institutions to a network of protocols.
A protocol does not set the content of decisions — it sets the conditions under which a decision is born. That is why it is never democratic, authoritarian, or liberal: those words belong to political systems, while a protocol describes only the mechanics of interaction. If the mechanics cuts losses — it scales; if it multiplies them — it localizes or vanishes. The evolution of protocols goes on without cease, and the civilization ceases to stabilize separate institutions — it stabilizes the very capacity to renew its own protocols without cease.
Cryptography opened protocols that no longer need a central guarantor. This is not a technological revolution but a change in the place where trust settles: proof slowly displaces authority, reproducibility — coercion, openness — monopoly. Homo Turensis takes these properties not as an end in themselves but as an instrument for holding conductivity between autonomous enclaves. The protocol ceases to be an auxiliary element and becomes the operating system of civilization.
The next law describes the mechanics of identity. Any protocol needs a node through which interaction enters the field of civilization — and Homo Turensis takes another model for this node. Not the citizen. Not the subject. Not the user. Not the consumer. The next unit of civilization becomes the operator of Spivbuttya.





Law VIII. Operator
Any civilization defines the human being through the function she carries within its mechanics. Homo Sapiens defines the human being as an individual, law — as a subject, the state — as a citizen, economics — as a producer, consumer, or owner, the corporation — as an employee, the social network — as a user. Each definition localizes only one function, and none describes the human being fully. The civilization interacts not with the human being — it interacts with the role it cut out itself.
The role grows out of the institution. The institution sets the permissible conduct, conduct — expectations, expectations — the social function, and the human being slowly begins to identify herself with the function the civilization holds. Identity shifts from process to category, the category outweighs interaction, and interaction adjusts itself to the category. So social inertia silts up: the civilization now coordinates not living processes but its own definitions.
Homo Turensis measures otherwise. The human being is defined not by status and not by role, but by the capacity to hold the conductivity of the field — a capacity that changes along with the environment, the scale of the task, accumulated experience, the structure of interaction. Identity ceases to be a fixed property and becomes a configuration of functions localized in a concrete moment. One person teaches, another researches, a third coordinates — and an hour later all three carry other functions. The personality does not lose wholeness. Only wholeness no longer holds by the immutability of the role.
The operator is the base unit of Homo Turensis. Not a post, not a profession, not a social status. An operator is a human being able to change her own function consciously, following the structure of the field, without losing inner coherence. The civilization ceases to demand immutability — it builds up the capacity for precise passage between modes of interaction.
The competence of an operator is measured not by the volume of information, because information ages quickly, but by the quality of coordination. The operator cuts the loss of interaction — the time between the appearance of a task and its localization, the number of intermediaries, the informational noise. And any decision is judged no longer by authorship but by how far it raised the conductivity of the field. The author slowly steps down from first place; the chief unit becomes the quality of interaction that arose after his act.
The operator does not represent himself. Each of his acts changes the configuration of the whole field of Spivbuttya, and any decision turns out to be at once local and civilizational. The line between the personal and the shared ceases to coincide with territory or right of ownership — now it is drawn by the scale of consequences. The field localizes through the operator; the operator localizes the responsibility of the field. Responsibility ceases to be a moral category and becomes a property of coordination.
Hence another understanding of freedom. Freedom is no longer the absence of constraints — any interaction already carries constraints within it. Freedom is the capacity to pass between functions without losing conductivity. The more configurations of interaction a human being is able to hold, the higher her operational freedom. Freedom ceases to be a political right and becomes a coordination characteristic.
The operator is not the final form of Homo Turensis but its smallest active unit. It is the interaction between operators that gives rise to the next level: if the operator localizes conductivity, then a group of operators localizes the consciousness of the civilization. From this arises the next law — the mechanics of collective consciousness.










Law IX. The Field That Thinks
Consciousness scales not through the accumulation of information but through coordination. More knowledge does not guarantee a greater capacity to arrive at reconciled decisions: information can multiply faster than understanding, data — faster than meaning, education — faster than conductivity between people. A civilization reaches a new scale not when it knows more, but when a greater number of consciousnesses begins to act as one coordination system without losing autonomy.
Collective consciousness is not a superorganism. It does not swallow the human being and does not stand in for individual thinking. It arises as a property of the interaction between operators who share a common ontology. As a single neuron holds no thinking of the brain, so a single operator holds no consciousness of the civilization: consciousness lives in the structure of the bonds, and a change of structure changes the properties of the whole field without needing to change each of its elements separately.
Separateness accumulates individual intellect. Turism accumulates the intellect of interaction — and this difference sets the next rung of evolution. Homo Sapiens learned to speed the thinking of a single human being. Homo Turensis speeds the thinking of a civilization. The human being ceases to be the final limit of the cognitive process and becomes one of the nodes through which the field becomes aware of itself.
Any decision passes through three levels. The first localizes in the operator, the second — in the enclave, the third — in the confederation, and each next does not cancel the one before it but only widens the horizon. The operator sees the local task, the enclave — the system of local tasks, the confederation — the interaction between systems. No level claims the absolute; fullness is born only from their simultaneous coordination.
The field does not think in place of the human being — it changes the quality of her thinking. The operator ceases to ask "what do I want to do?" and begins to ask another thing: "what configuration of interaction does the field localize through me?" The answer is no longer sought inside the separate personality — it shows through at the point of intersection of the whole configuration of Spivbuttya. The ego ceases to be the center of decision and becomes one of the sensors of the field.
That is why Homo Turensis holds to another ethics. What becomes ethical is not the intention but the act that raises the conductivity of the civilization. There is no universal list of right deeds — there is only one regularity: any act either raises the conductivity of the field or multiplies its inner resistance. Ethics ceases to be a system of prohibitions and becomes a physics of interaction.
The individual "I" does not vanish in this — it changes its own scale. The human being still has a body, memory, character, history, her own inimitable way of perceiving. Only the mechanics of self-identification changes: the operator ceases to define himself as a closed entity and begins to see himself as a local form through which the field of Spivbuttya observes itself. The other human being ceases to be an external object and becomes another localization of the same process.
Hence the chief principle of Homo Turensis. Any interaction is an interaction of the field with itself; any conflict is a conflict of two localizations of one process; any learning is a transmission of information within one consciousness, spilled across many forms. The civilization ceases to accumulate population and begins to accumulate the degree of its own self-awareness.
It is from this law that the next arises. If a civilization becomes aware of itself as a single field, the nature of the economy changes too: exchange ceases to be a transfer of resources between independent sides and becomes a way of circulating value within one process of Spivbuttya.







Law X. Circulation
Value does not lie inside the object — it is born in the very process of interaction. A stone has no value in itself, gold has no value in itself, data have no value in themselves, and the human being, too, has no fixed value. Value flares up in that configuration of Spivbuttya where interaction raises the conductivity of the field. Any object, knowledge, or act takes on weight only when it enters coordination.
The economy of Homo Sapiens accumulates the right to value. Ownership localizes control over a resource, capital — over production, money — the right to a future exchange. Each of these mechanisms once raised productivity by cheapening coordination — but along with productivity another property came running: value slowly tore free of the interaction that gave rise to it. Exchange ceased to be the completion of cooperation and became a separate sphere, alive already in itself.
Turism flows otherwise. Value does not accumulate — it circulates. Any attempt to halt the flow and lock it in on itself lowers the conductivity of the system; any circulation that raises conductivity multiplies the total value of the field. The economy ceases to be defined by the volume of accumulated assets and begins to be defined by the speed, quality, and precision with which value flows between operators, enclaves, the confederation — as standing water silts up, while flowing water stays alive.
So wealth, too, ceases to be the quantity of controlled resources. Wealth is the capacity to give rise to interactions after which the conductivity of the civilization grows. A person who ties trust between dozens of enclaves enriches the civilization regardless of her own account. A researcher who opens a new mechanics of coordination enriches it regardless of the price of a patent. An architect who draws a space that strengthens Spivbuttya enriches it regardless of the price of the building. Value ceases to measure the object and begins to measure the change of the state of the field after interaction.
Money in this does not vanish — its function changes. It descends from the place of the chief store of value and becomes a protocol of its movement. A means of coordination cannot set the nature of what it coordinates: as language does not make an utterance true, so money does not make an interaction valuable — it only fixes one of its dimensions. And a civilization that confuses money with value slowly takes to polishing its own measuring instrument instead of the process it was meant to measure.
Hence another architecture of the economy. Capital ceases to be a static heap of resources and becomes a store of future coordination capacity. Investment ceases to be a placement into profit and becomes a placement into the conductivity of the civilization. Profit ceases to be the final goal and becomes a signal — a sign that a certain mechanics of interaction turned out more effective than the one before. And the moment profit ceases to coincide with the growth of conductivity, it slowly loses its civilizational function, however large it stays.
The DAO, cryptography, open protocols, distributed ledgers take on a new sense here. They do not create a new economy — they cut the loss during the circulation of value, remove the intermediaries between the appearance of interaction and its embodiment. Technology ceases to be a source of economic advantage and becomes a mechanism for raising the conductivity of the economy.
Homo Turensis does not deny the market — it changes its place. The market descends from the center of civilization and becomes one of the mechanisms of coordination within a far wider field of Spivbuttya. No mechanism claims universality; each is taken up where it raises conductivity. That is why the civilization ceases to divide itself into economy, politics, culture, education, technology — all of them turn out to be only different regimes of the current of one and the same value.
The next law describes the mechanics of time. A civilization changes not when it accumulates more resources, but when it begins to accumulate the future differently.



Law XI. Time
Time measures not the duration of a civilization but the horizon of its coordination. Any system makes decisions within the future it is able to hold before its eyes. A short horizon — and the civilization optimizes the immediate result; a long one — and it optimizes the conditions under which coming generations will keep the capacity to coordinate. The future is not a moment that has not yet come. The future is the scale of interaction that already now presses on the present.
Each institution carries its own horizon. The market optimizes the nearest cycle of exchange, the corporation — the next financial period, the state — the political cycle, science — a generation of researchers, biological evolution — millennia. The civilization coordinates all these horizons at once, and as long as they are compatible, the current runs even. The moment they diverge, an inner resistance grows: short-term decisions begin to undermine long-term conductivity, and the system, still working, slowly spends its own future on the upkeep of the present.
The horizon of time is set by ontology. Homo Sapiens accumulates the future through the preservation of form: the state strives to preserve the state, the company — the company, the family — the family. Turism accumulates the future through the preservation of mechanics. Forms change ceaselessly, the mechanics stays compatible. An enclave may arise, transform, go out — the conductivity between enclaves flows on. An institution may reach the end of its cycle — the protocol pours over into a new form. The civilization ceases to identify its own continuity with the continuity of separate structures.
So any decision turns out to be an investment into a certain horizon. If it raises the productivity of the present at the price of the conductivity of the future, the civilization accumulates a hidden debt; if it sacrifices local effectiveness for the sake of long-term coordination capacity, it accumulates future freedom. Debt ceases to be a financial category and becomes the difference between the present level of coordination and the level the field will be able to hold after the consequences of today's decisions.
Memory, too, changes its function. The archive exists no longer to store information but to store mechanics: the civilization accumulates not texts for the sake of texts, but ways of coordination that at least once already raised conductivity. And each generation no longer repeats the path of the one before — it gains ready access to mechanics already tested by interaction. Evolution speeds not through the increase of knowledge but through the shortening of the time between the discovery of a new mechanics and its localization by the whole confederation.
So education, too, ceases to be a transmission of information between generations and becomes a transmission of the ways of holding the field. The child inherits no longer only language, craft, or history — she inherits the architecture of interaction. And each generation gains the right to change the mechanics, if the new configuration raises the conductivity of the civilization. Tradition ceases to mean the repetition of the past; tradition means the continuity of evolution.
Even immortality changes its meaning. What becomes immortal is no longer the human being and not the institution, but the mechanics that goes on localizing through new generations, new enclaves, new forms of life. The civilization ceases to measure its own age in years and begins to measure it by the number of successful passages between forms without loss of ontology. Time no longer destroys a civilization — time only takes away the mechanics that have lost the capacity to coordinate the future.
Hence the last principle of Homo Turensis. The civilization is built no longer for its contemporaries — it localizes a field of Spivbuttya able to coordinate consciousness independently of generation, territory, biological form, or technological carrier. The future ceases to be a continuation of the present and becomes the next localization of the same ontology.
The next law completes the mechanics. If ontology sets the civilization, coordination — its scale, and conductivity — its development, one last question remains: on what condition does a civilization pass into the next state. This question sets the mechanics of the phase transition.


Law XII. The Phase Transition
Civilizations do not collapse — they pass between mechanics of coordination. The outside observer sees wars, economic shifts, technological revolutions, political conflicts. Within, another process runs: the previous ontology slowly ceases to give the needed coordination capacity, the new one already localizes inside the old civilization but does not yet set its architecture — and for a while both mechanics flow side by side, in one riverbed. The phase transition begins in the moment when the new mechanics coordinates interaction more cheaply than the previous one.
The transition never engulfs the whole civilization at once. It flares up in the nodes of highest conductivity: new ways of coordination arise locally, pass the test of interaction, and if the loss is cut — the mechanics takes to flowing outward. It is not ideology that flows but the very capacity of a system to hold greater complexity at lesser cost. Any successful civilizational change happens so: it does not persuade the previous mechanics — it slowly makes it needless.
So the phase transition begins not with a change of power, not with a change of economy, and not even with a change of culture. It begins in the moment when enough operators begin to coordinate interaction by the new ontology. Institutions catch up later. Law only fixes the already-formed mechanics, politics catches up with the already-formed coordination, economics begins to circulate by the already-formed conductivity. A civilization always changes its own names after it has already changed its own physics.
So Homo Turensis, too, comes not by revolution. Revolution changes the center — Homo Turensis changes the very principle by which the center is localized. Reform changes the institution — Homo Turensis changes the mechanics from which institutions are born at all. Elections change the participants of coordination — Homo Turensis changes the unit of coordination. None of these processes contradicts another: they simply live on different levels of the civilizational architecture.
The first bearers of the transition are the enclaves. They localize the mechanics of Spivbuttya in a space where it can be tested without recutting the whole civilization at once: each enclave becomes an experiment, each experiment bears new protocols, each protocol passes the test of interaction, and the confederation accumulates only the mechanics that raise the conductivity of the field. Error ceases to be a threat and becomes a local piece of news about the limits of a concrete mechanics. Evolution no longer holds by faultlessness — it holds by the speed of learning.
The phase transition ends not when the new civilization engulfs the majority of the population, but when the previous ontology ceases to be a needed condition of coordination. In that moment people may still walk about with the old names, the old states, the old currencies, the old languages. The mechanics will already be different. The forms will change later — the architecture will change earlier.
Homo Turensis does not describe a future state, does not describe a new religion, does not describe a political doctrine. It describes the mechanics of a civilization that arises after Homo Sapiens as a consequence of the passage to the ontology of Spivbuttya. Its development holds not by centralization but by conductivity; not by accumulation but by circulation; not by immutability but by ceaseless localization; not by control but by coordination; not by isolated entities but by a field of interaction.
This document does not complete the mechanics of Homo Turensis — it only localizes its initial configuration. Any mechanics lives as long as it is able to surpass its own previous form, and so Homo Turensis is not the end of history. Homo Turensis is the passage of a civilization into the regime of ceaseless evolution of its own mechanics.
The next document begins where this one ends. If the mechanics is defined, the next step is its embodiment. The architecture of enclaves. The protocols of the confederation. The economy of conductivity. The education of operators. Pupsocracy. Cryptographic identity. The planetary network of Spivbuttya. The mechanics is complete.  
The engineering begins.
